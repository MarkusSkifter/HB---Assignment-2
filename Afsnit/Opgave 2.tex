\section{Question 2: Why Is Behavior Unchanged at $\mu = 0.5$?}

When we introduced the weights in Question~1, the entire weighted utility was scaled by a factor of 2 so that the consumption term stays identical to the baseline. This means the only terms affected by $\mu$ are the labor-disutility terms, which are multiplied by $2\mu$ and $2(1-\mu)$ respectively.

At $\mu = 0.5$ these factors become $2 \cdot 0.5 = 1$ and $2(1-0.5) = 1$, so the weighted utility reduces exactly to the baseline:
%
\begin{align}\label{eq:mu_half}
    U &= 2\frac{(c_t/2)^{1+\eta}}{1+\eta}
       - 2\mu \, \rho_1 \frac{h_{1,t}^{1+\gamma}}{1+\gamma}
       - 2(1-\mu) \, \rho_2 \frac{h_{2,t}^{1+\gamma}}{1+\gamma} \notag \\
      &= 2\frac{(c_t/2)^{1+\eta}}{1+\eta}
       - \rho_1 \frac{h_{1,t}^{1+\gamma}}{1+\gamma}
       - \rho_2 \frac{h_{2,t}^{1+\gamma}}{1+\gamma}
\end{align}
%
which is identical to the baseline utility in equation~\eqref{eq:baseline_util}. Because the objective function is the same, the optimizer chooses the same hours $h_1$ and $h_2$ in every period, and therefore all simulated labor-market outcomes are unchanged.
